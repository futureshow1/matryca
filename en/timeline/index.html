Timeline — sixteen scenes

Timeline

From Facebook Beacon in 2007, when users first publicly protested being made subjects of their own shopping, to localhost in 2025, when Meta and Yandex apps were shown to bypass every browser safeguard to keep tracking in incognito mode.

Interactive scrollytelling version with ~12,000 words of narrative is currently available in the Polish edition. English summaries follow. Full translation planned for Phase 4.

  1. 2007

    Facebook Beacon — the first crack

    Facebook launched a shopping-activity broadcast feature without opt-in. Public protest, a MoveOn petition with 50,000 signatures in five days, and a $9.5M Lane v. Facebook settlement followed. The first documented stress test of "move fast, apologize after".

  2. 2010

    Hamburg tells Google: show us

    Johannes Caspar, Hamburg's Commissioner for Data Protection, forced Google to admit that Street View cars had harvested fragments of open Wi-Fi traffic — passwords, emails, URLs — across 30+ countries over three years. First credible enforcement of European data law against a US giant.

  3. 2012

    Google bypasses Safari. Instagram costs $1B.

    Google circumvented Safari's default third-party cookie block via a hidden form trick. $22.5M FTC fine — then a record. The same year Facebook acquired Instagram for $1B on a weekend email; eight years later the FTC filed antitrust charges.

  4. 2013

    Snowden hands journalists 1.5 million documents

    The Guardian and Washington Post began publishing on June 5. PRISM revealed. Nine US tech firms named. The European debate around data transfers restarted in earnest — leading to Schrems I (2015), GDPR (2016), Schrems II (2020).

  5. 2014

    689,003 users without informed consent

    Facebook manipulated the News Feeds of 689,003 users for one week in 2012 to study emotional contagion. Published in PNAS in June 2014. No fines; but the debate about platform-scale human experimentation without consent — still active today — began here.

  6. 2015

    Schrems I — Safe Harbor falls

    27-year-old Max Schrems, the Austrian law student, forced the European Court of Justice to invalidate the 15-year-old Safe Harbor framework. Thousands of US firms suddenly had to redesign their data transfers. Schrems then founded NOYB to keep pushing.

  7. 2016

    GDPR is adopted

    The European Parliament adopted the General Data Protection Regulation on April 14. Maximum fines of 4% global turnover. Two-year grace period until May 25, 2018. Most firms made cosmetic adjustments; fundamental product rearchitecture was delayed.

  8. 2017

    Uber's Greyball. Equifax's 147 million.

    Uber's Greyball tool deceived regulators for years. Susan Fowler's February blog post triggered the Kalanick ouster. Equifax disclosed a breach of 147 million Americans' data in September — payout per victim ultimately averaged $6.90.

  9. 2018

    Cambridge Analytica breaks the surface

    The March Observer/NYT series revealed 87 million Facebook profiles harvested via a personality-test app and used for psychographic targeting in Trump 2016 and Brexit. FTC fine: $5B (July 2019). Facebook stock rose 1.8% the week after.

  10. 2019

    FTC collects billions. Children finally matter.

    FTC $5.1B Facebook settlement. YouTube $170M COPPA fine. Capital One breach: 100 million records. The year American regulators stopped fining with thousands and started fining with billions.

  11. 2020

    Faces cannot be turned off

    Kashmir Hill's NYT exposé on Clearview AI revealed 3+ billion scraped photos sold to US police. The same year Zoom's claimed "end-to-end encryption" was debunked by Citizen Lab. Pandemic made these platforms unavoidable.

  12. 2021

    Haugen carries out 22,000 pages

    Frances Haugen testified before the Senate with Facebook internal research showing Instagram harmed teen girls. Same year: the Pegasus Project revealed NSO spyware on journalists, activists, heads of state. Apple tried on-device CSAM scanning, backed down in three weeks.

  13. 2022

    Musk buys Twitter. Mudge Zatko testifies.

    Elon Musk completed the $44B Twitter acquisition in October. Half the staff was laid off. Peiter "Mudge" Zatko, former Twitter security chief, filed SEC/FTC/DOJ disclosures alleging "extreme" vulnerabilities. Sephora received the first CCPA fine ($1.2M).

  14. 2023

    GDPR becomes a number, not a letter

    Irish DPC fined Meta €1.2B for transfers to the US — the largest GDPR fine in history. €390M for behavioral-ads basis. €345M against TikTok for children's data. Meta's reaction: 1.8% stock increase. 0.7% of annual revenue. "Fine as cost of business" — confirmed.

  15. 2024

    AI trains on us without asking. Clearview Italy — again.

    LinkedIn activated AI training on user posts and messages, opt-out only. €310M Irish DPC fine. Italian Garante issued its second €20M fine against Clearview AI for continuing to scrape EU citizen faces. EU Digital Markets Act took effect.

  16. 2025

    Browser sandboxing — bypassed. Incognito — bypassed.

    IMDEA Networks / Radboud / KU Leuven disclosed Meta and Yandex apps using localhost sockets to re-identify incognito sessions. Ten years of practice. Three hours to switch off. First DMA fine: €200M against Meta. €530M against TikTok for transfers to China.

Decision game — five scenarios

What would you do?

History tends to turn on specific decisions by specific people in specific rooms. Here are five real ones. Each is described from the point of view of the person in the room — before they knew how it would end.

Interactive game is currently available in the Polish edition only. English scenarios below are summaries. The full interactive version — with four choices, consequence text, and scoring — is planned for Phase 4.

  1. Scenario 1 · 2021

    Frances Haugen's decision

    You work as a product manager in Meta's Civic Integrity team. You have seen internal research — Instagram harms teen girls, the algorithm promotes anger, XCheck protects 5.8 million VIP accounts from moderation. Management reads your reports and changes nothing. Your contract says disclosure is a crime. What do you do?

  2. Scenario 2 · 2011

    Max Schrems' question

    You are a 23-year-old law student in Vienna on exchange in Silicon Valley. You ask Facebook — under Irish law — what data they have on you. They send you 1,200 pages: everything, including messages you deleted, friends you blocked, photos you untagged. You can let it go. Or you can do something. What?

  3. Scenario 3 · 2010

    Johannes Caspar's audit

    You are Commissioner for Data Protection in Hamburg. Budget: €3M. Staff: 18. Google Street View cars are driving through your city. You notice an antenna on the roof and ask Google what it does. They say "Wi-Fi names only". You could accept that. Or you could push. And push again.

  4. Scenario 4 · 2018

    Christopher Wylie's disclosure

    You are 28, you worked at Cambridge Analytica as research director, you saw 87 million Facebook profiles harvested for psychographic targeting in Trump 2016 and Brexit. The firm's NDAs promise legal war. Journalists — Carole Cadwalladr, The Observer — are asking for a source. What do you tell them?

  5. Scenario 5 · 2018

    The Dragonfly engineer

    You are a Google engineer on Project Dragonfly. The project is secret: a censored search engine for China. It will filter "human rights", "Tiananmen", "Dalai Lama". Your paycheck pays your mortgage. Your NDA is strict. But The Intercept is calling. And 1,400 of your colleagues have already signed an internal letter of protest.
